Bernard Moitessier in the documentary Deep Water that features the Sunday Times Golden Globe regatta. The first solo-non-stop-circumnavigation regatta.

Subtle qualities

"The sheet winches creak, the water murmurs on the bottom as Joshua gathers way and begins to come alive... People who do not know that a sailboat is a living creature will never understand anything about boats and the sea.
The wake stretches on and on, white and dense with life by day, luminous by night. Like long tresses of dreams and stars. Water runs along the hull and rumbles or sings or rustles, depending on the wind, depending on the sky, depending on wether the sund was setting red or grey. For many days it has been red, and the wind hums in the rigging, makes a halyard tap against the mast at times, passes over the sails like caress and goes on its way to the west, toward Madeira, as Joshua rushes to the south in the trade wind at 7 knots.
Wind, sea, boat and sails, a compact, diffuse whole, without beginning or end, a part and all of the universe... my own universe, truly mine.
I watch the sun set and inhale the breath of the open sea, I feel my being blossoming and my joy soars so high that nothing can disturb it. The other questions, the ones that used to bother me at times, do not weigh anything before the immensity of a wake so close to the sky and filled with the wind of the sea."

The Seagull and the old ship

"The weather is fine; The wake runs off behind me with a slight ripple. Sitting in the cockpit, I watch the sea and listen to the music of the water thrown by the stern. And I see a little seagull descend on my knee. I don't dare move, not even breathe, for fear that it might fly away and not return. It is completely white, almost translucent, with very large, black eyes and a finely cut beak. I hadn't seen it fly up, hadn't felt its wing flapping faster as it settled down. My body is naked in the sun, and yet I don't feel it on the skin of my knee. It weighs next to nothing. My hand approaches her slowly. She looks at me while cleaning her plumage. I'm getting closer She stops cleaning herself and looks at me without fear. And her eyes seem to be talking to me. My hand approaches her a little more gently to caress her back. Now she speaks to me, and at that moment I understand that it is by no means a miracle, but a perfectly natural thing. And she tells me the story of the beautiful sailing ship, manned by human beings. With millions of human beings. On departure they spoke of a long research trip. People wanted to know where they came from, where they were going. But over time they completely forgot why they were actually on the ship. Then, and I begin, very, very gradually, they got fat and became demanding passengers who were no longer interested in the life of the sea and the ship. What interested her was only her small, personal comfort. They have submitted to becoming mediocre, and when they say "That's life" they are professing resignation and lack of resistance. Even the captain has resigned for fear of upsetting his passengers if he turned to avoid the unknown reefs, the threat of which he senses in the depth of his instinct. The visibility gets worse, the wind increases, and the beautiful sailing ship sails on the same course. The captain hopes a miracle will occur that will soothe the sea and allow him to turn without bothering anyone. The sun has risen to midday. She crossed the meridian without my moving. Now my seagull is sleeping on my knee. I've known her for a long time. It is the white seagull that lives on all islands where the sun is the god of men. She flies out to sea in the morning and always returns to her island in the evening. So it is enough to follow it."

Embrace the suck

"Energy-sapping storms, dangerously high waves, gloomy clouds that scurry over the water and carry all the sadness, all the despondency of the world. Still, you keep going, perhaps because you know you have to keep going, even if you don't understand why. Bright skies and blood-red sunsets over a lake that sparkles with power and light and gives you all its strength, its whole truth. Then you know why you are going on, why you want to go to the end and go further. Rushing of the water Rushing of the water. The rush of water Once upon a time the alchemist worked on matter for a long, long, long time. A very, very, very long time. And people believed that he was out to create the miracle stone that would turn all things into gold. What the alchemist was really looking for was not the miracle stone that turns all things into gold. It was just the transformation of the alchemist himself, through time, patience, and more time. And sometimes the alchemist went too far. But in the high latitudes, people who are crushed by their smallness are at the same time supported and protected by the feeling of their size. Right there, in the infinite ode of the southern ocean, I clearly feel how much man is atom and god at the same time. And when I go on deck at dawn, it can happen that I want to scream out my joie de vivre at the sight of the sky that lights up over the long, foam-crowned waves of this gigantic sea, an expression of power and beauty that sometimes tries to murder. With my whole being I live what is called living. And looking at the sea, it may be inevitable to go further."
